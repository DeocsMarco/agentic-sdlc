## 1. Purpose

This work instruction guides the AM and C-Suite through developing a project strategy and transforming it into a client-ready proposal. It covers everything from defining the delivery approach through proposal submission and follow-up.

The two activities — strategy and proposal — are combined because the strategy exists to inform the proposal. Doing them back-to-back ensures alignment and reduces rework.

---

## 2. Scope

**Applies To**: Account Manager (AM) with C-Suite collaboration

**When to Use**: After WI-MGT-001 produces a "Go" recommendation approved by C-Suite

**Time Required**: 8–16 hours total (4–8 hours strategy development, 4–8 hours proposal preparation and submission)

**Responsible**: AM + C-Suite | **Accountable**: C-Suite

## 5. Procedure

### Part A: Develop Project Strategy

#### Step 2.1: Define Project Approach

**Objective**: Establish how we'll deliver this project.

1. **Select methodology** based on project characteristics:
   - **Agile (Scrum)**: Iterative, evolving requirements, high client collaboration
   - **Waterfall**: Fixed scope, clear requirements, regulatory constraints
   - **Hybrid**: Mix of fixed milestones with iterative development within phases
   - Document rationale — why this methodology for this client and project

2. **Define project phases** (typically 3–5):
   - For each phase: name, duration, key activities, deliverables
   - Align phases with client milestones from RFP
   - Include quality gates at phase boundaries

3. **Specify technology stack**:
   - Frontend framework, backend language/framework, database, infrastructure
   - For each choice: rationale, version, team familiarity
   - Flag any technologies requiring training or contractor support

4. **Describe change management process**:
   - How scope changes are requested, evaluated, and approved
   - Impact assessment process for timeline and budget

Document in PLAN-STRATEGY-001, Sections 1–4.

---

#### Step 2.2: Plan Resources & Timeline

**Objective**: Define who's doing the work and when.

1. **Design team structure**:
   - List all roles needed (PM, developers, QA, designer, DevOps, etc.)
   - Specify allocation percentage per role
   - Assign names where known; flag positions to be filled
   - Verify allocations match the budget in FORM-BUDGET-ASSESSMENT-001

2. **Define escalation path**:
   - Level 1: PM (day-to-day)
   - Level 2: Tech Lead / PM Lead (technical/resource)
   - Level 3: C-Suite (strategic, budget, client relationship)

3. **Establish milestones** (typically 4–6):
   - For each: target date, success criteria, dependencies
   - Identify the critical path — which delays cascade?
   - Verify milestones meet client's required dates with adequate buffer

4. **Define client engagement model**:
   - Communication frequency and method per audience (sponsor, stakeholders, tech team)
   - Status reporting cadence and format
   - Feedback loops (sprint reviews, UAT sessions, milestone reviews)
   - Client responsibilities and expected response times

Document in PLAN-STRATEGY-001, Sections 5–7.

---

#### Step 2.3: Address Risks and Assumptions

**Objective**: Don't pretend risks don't exist. Name them and plan for them.

1. **Review risks from FORM-FEASIBILITY-001** — any dimension that scored below 4 has associated risks
2. **Brainstorm additional risks** with C-Suite across categories:
   - Technical, resource, schedule, budget, client, external
3. **For each significant risk** (top 5–8):
   - Probability (High/Medium/Low) and Impact (High/Medium/Low)
   - Mitigation strategy (what we'll do to prevent it)
   - Contingency plan (what we'll do if it happens anyway)
   - Owner
4. **Document assumptions** — things we're treating as true but haven't verified:
   - Client assumptions (timely decisions, system access, SME availability)
   - Resource assumptions (team stability, contractor delivery)
   - Technical assumptions (API availability, infrastructure support)
   - Budget assumptions (no major scope changes, stable costs)
5. **Document constraints** — known limitations (fixed budget, hard deadlines, regulatory)

Document in PLAN-STRATEGY-001, Sections 8–10.

---

#### Step 2.4: Obtain Strategy Approval

**Objective**: Get C-Suite sign-off before investing time in the proposal.

1. Review PLAN-STRATEGY-001 for completeness and internal consistency
2. Verify alignment with feasibility and budget assessments
3. Present to C-Suite (30–60 minute review meeting recommended)
4. Address questions or concerns; revise if needed
5. Obtain C-Suite approval signature on PLAN-STRATEGY-001

**Approval criteria**:
- All sections complete with adequate detail
- Strategy addresses all client RFP requirements
- Resources, timeline, and budget are feasible
- High/medium risks have mitigation plans
- C-Suite, AM, and PM Lead agree on approach

**If not approved**: Revise and resubmit. If fundamental issues arise, re-evaluate the Go decision.

---

### Part B: Prepare and Submit Proposal

#### Step 2.5: Draft the Proposal

**Objective**: Transform strategy into a compelling client-facing document.

Open TMPL-PROPOSAL-001 and complete all sections:

1. **Cover page** — Company logo, project title, client info, dates, proposal validity period (30–90 days)
2. **Executive summary** (200–400 words) — Client's challenge, our solution, why us, expected outcomes. Adapt from PLAN-STRATEGY-001 Section 1 using the client's own language from the RFP.
3. **Scope of work** — Methodology, phases, technology stack, out-of-scope items. Transfer from strategy.
4. **Deliverables** — Table with ID, name, description, acceptance criteria, target date for each deliverable.
5. **Timeline & milestones** — Transfer from strategy. Include visual timeline if possible.
6. **Pricing & budget** — Cost breakdown from FORM-BUDGET-ASSESSMENT-001. Present in client-friendly format. Clearly state inclusions and exclusions.
7. **Payment terms** — Schedule (e.g., 25% deposit, milestone payments, final payment), invoicing terms, accepted methods.
8. **Acceptance criteria** — How deliverables will be reviewed, tested, and accepted. UAT process.
9. **Assumptions** — Transfer from strategy. These protect both parties.
10. **Terms & conditions** — IP, confidentiality, warranties, liability, termination. Review against company standard T&Cs.
11. **Signature page** — Both parties' authorized signatories with proposal reference and total value.

---

#### Step 2.6: Quality Review

**Objective**: Catch errors before the client does.

Run through these checks:

- ☐ Client company name spelled correctly throughout (use Find to verify)
- ☐ No placeholder text remaining (search for "[" and "TBD")
- ☐ All dates are consistent and in logical sequence
- ☐ Pricing calculations are correct (double-check arithmetic)
- ☐ Every RFP requirement is addressed somewhere in the proposal
- ☐ Pricing matches FORM-BUDGET-ASSESSMENT-001
- ☐ Formatting is consistent (fonts, headings, spacing, branding)
- ☐ Grammar and spelling checked (run spell-check + read key sections aloud)
- ☐ Page count and format meet RFP submission requirements

**Tip**: Have a second pair of eyes review the proposal. Fresh perspective catches what you've become blind to.

---

#### Step 2.7: C-Suite Final Approval

**Objective**: Last gate before the proposal leaves the building.

1. Submit proposal draft to C-Suite with PLAN-STRATEGY-001 and FORM-BUDGET-ASSESSMENT-001 for reference
2. Request review within 2 business days
3. Incorporate feedback and adjust as needed
4. Obtain final sign-off — confirm authorized signatory is available if client accepts

---

#### Step 2.8: Submit to Client

**Objective**: Professional delivery, on time, via the right channel.

1. **Pre-submission checks**:
   - ☐ Proposal exported to PDF with professional formatting
   - ☐ All required attachments prepared (case studies, resumes, certifications)
   - ☐ File size within client limits
   - ☐ Submission method confirmed (email, portal, hand delivery)
   - ☐ Adequate buffer before deadline (submit at least 4 hours early)

2. **Submit** via client-specified method:
   - **Email**: Professional cover message, attach PDF and attachments, request read receipt
   - **Portal**: Upload all documents, complete metadata fields, capture confirmation
   - **Physical**: Professional packaging, courier with tracking and signature

3. **Post-submission**:
   - Verify delivery (check sent folder, delivery receipt, portal confirmation)
   - Send confirmation follow-up within 24 hours — brief email confirming receipt, offering to present or answer questions
   - Set calendar reminders: follow-up date (5–7 days), proposal expiration date, expected decision date
   - Track proposal status: Pending → Awarded / Declined

**If Awarded** → Proceed to SOP Step 3 (Contract Signing)
**If Declined** → Document reason, conduct brief lessons-learned, file for reference

## 8. Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why It Happens | How to Avoid It |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Strategy and proposal contradict each other** | Written at different times, not cross-checked | Do them back-to-back. Final step: verify proposal matches strategy. |
| **Generic, boilerplate proposal** | Copy-paste from last proposal without customizing | Use client's language from the RFP. Reference their specific problems and goals. |
| **Pricing doesn't match budget assessment** | Manual transfer errors | Always copy numbers from FORM-BUDGET-ASSESSMENT-001. Triple-check totals. |
| **Missing the submission deadline** | Started too late, underestimated effort | Block time on day 1. Proposal prep takes 4–8 hours minimum. |
| **Over-engineering the strategy** | Trying to plan everything upfront | Strategy informs the proposal, not the project plan. Keep it directional, not exhaustive. |
| **Skipping C-Suite review** | "They're busy" or "it's straightforward" | Never skip. C-Suite catches strategic misalignment you're too close to see. |
| **No follow-up after submission** | Assuming the client will call when ready | The 24-hour confirmation + 5–7 day check-in are non-negotiable touchpoints. |

---

## 9. Tips for Success

- **Strategy first, proposal second**: Don't start writing the proposal until the strategy is approved. It's tempting to parallelize, but strategy changes = proposal rework.
- **Use the strategy as your outline**: PLAN-STRATEGY-001 contains 80% of the proposal content. Adapt, don't rewrite from scratch.
- **Know your audience**: The proposal is read by business decision-makers, not just technical people. Lead with value, not features.
- **Less is more in pricing**: Present a clear total with a simple breakdown. Avoid 15-row spreadsheets that invite line-item negotiation.
- **Proofread the cover page**: The first thing the client sees. One typo there kills credibility for everything after.
