## Purpose

This form guides the development of mitigation strategies for high-priority risks (score ≥6 from WORK-PM-002). It documents response strategies, assigns ownership, establishes timelines, and tracks implementation progress. This ensures high-priority risks have actionable plans to reduce their probability or impact.

**Used By**: Project Manager (with risk owners and team input)
**Time Required**: 45-60 minutes per high-priority risk

---

## Methodology Overview

Risk mitigation uses four standard response strategies: **Avoid, Reduce, Transfer, or Accept**. For risks requiring mitigation (score ≥6), a detailed action plan is created with specific tasks, owners, and success criteria.

**Key Principles**:
1. **Prevention is better than reaction**: Focus on reducing probability or impact before risk occurs
2. **Assign clear ownership**: Every mitigation action needs a specific person accountable
3. **Define success criteria**: How will we know if mitigation worked?
4. **Monitor continuously**: Mitigation is not "set and forget"; track progress weekly

**Steps**:
1. Select high-priority risk from WORK-PM-002 (score ≥6)
2. Choose appropriate mitigation strategy (Avoid, Reduce, Transfer, Accept)
3. Develop specific mitigation actions with owners and timelines
4. Document contingency plan (what to do if risk occurs despite mitigation)
5. Track implementation progress and measure effectiveness

## Reference Tables

### Table 1: Risk Response Strategies

| Strategy | Definition | When to Use | Example |
|----------|------------|-------------|---------|
| **Avoid** | Eliminate the risk entirely by changing approach | High impact risks that can be eliminated | Use proven technology instead of experimental framework |
| **Reduce (Mitigate)** | Lower probability or impact through proactive actions | Most common strategy for manageable risks | Cross-train team members to reduce key person dependency |
| **Transfer** | Shift risk to third party (insurance, vendor, contract) | Risks where others are better equipped to manage | Use SLA with penalty clauses for vendor delivery |
| **Accept** | Acknowledge risk but take no proactive action | Low priority risks or unavoidable risks with low impact | Accept minor budget variance risk if within 5% contingency |

**Usage**: For each high-priority risk, select the most appropriate strategy based on cost-effectiveness and feasibility.

---

### Table 2: Mitigation Action Quality Checklist

| Quality Criterion | Good Example | Poor Example |
|-------------------|--------------|--------------|
| **Specific** | "Conduct knowledge transfer sessions every Friday for 4 weeks" | "Document the code" |
| **Measurable** | "Cross-train 2 developers to 80% proficiency by Feb 15" | "Train the team" |
| **Actionable** | "Purchase backup API subscription; implement failover by Jan 30" | "Have a backup plan" |
| **Time-bound** | "Complete security audit by Feb 1; remediate findings by Feb 15" | "Address security concerns" |

**Usage**: Validate each mitigation action against SMART criteria (Specific, Measurable, Actionable, Relevant, Time-bound).

## Common Mitigation Errors & Solutions

| Error | Cause | Solution |
|-------|-------|----------|
| Mitigation actions are vague ("monitor closely") | Lack of specific planning | Ask "What specific action will we take? Who does it? By when?" |
| No owner assigned or owner is "team" | Avoiding accountability | Assign to specific individual; confirm they accept responsibility |
| Mitigation cost exceeds risk impact | Over-engineering the response | Calculate cost-benefit; consider accepting low-cost risks instead |
| Contingency plan missing or unrealistic | "Hope for the best" mindset | Force question: "If this happens tomorrow, what's our first action?" |
| Actions never completed; perpetual "in progress" | No enforcement or follow-up | Add to weekly status reviews; escalate blockers immediately |

## Mitigation Strategy Examples

### Example 1: Avoid Strategy

**Risk**: "Experimental AI framework may have bugs causing system instability"
**Score**: 9 (High probability × High impact)

**Mitigation Strategy**: Avoid - Use proven framework instead
**Actions**:
1. Evaluate mature alternatives (TensorFlow, PyTorch) by Jan 15 - Owner: Tech Lead
2. Prototype with selected proven framework by Jan 25 - Owner: Dev Team
3. Complete migration from experimental to proven framework by Feb 5 - Owner: Tech Lead

**Result**: Risk eliminated; new risk score = 0 (risk no longer exists)

---

### Example 2: Reduce Strategy

**Risk**: "Key developer may leave mid-project due to competing offer"
**Score**: 9 (High probability × High impact)

**Mitigation Strategy**: Reduce - Cross-train team and document knowledge
**Actions**:
1. Conduct weekly knowledge transfer sessions (4 weeks) - Owner: Key Developer
2. Cross-train 2 developers to 80% proficiency - Owner: Tech Lead
3. Document critical architecture decisions and codebase structure - Owner: Key Developer
4. Negotiate retention bonus with HR - Owner: PM

**Result**: Risk reduced to score 4 (Medium probability × Medium impact - team can continue with moderate delay if developer leaves)

---

### Example 3: Transfer Strategy

**Risk**: "Third-party vendor may deliver component late, delaying project"
**Score**: 6 (Medium probability × High impact)

**Mitigation Strategy**: Transfer - Add contract penalties and SLAs
**Actions**:
1. Negotiate SLA with penalty clause ($10K per week late) - Owner: PM
2. Require vendor to post performance bond - Owner: Contracts Manager
3. Add contract clause allowing alternate vendor sourcing if >2 weeks late - Owner: Legal

**Result**: Risk transferred; vendor incentivized to deliver on time; project protected financially if delay occurs

---

### Example 4: Accept Strategy

**Risk**: "Client may request minor UI color scheme changes late in project"
**Score**: 4 (Medium probability × Low impact)

**Mitigation Strategy**: Accept - Budget allows for minor changes; no proactive action
**Actions**: None (Monitor only)

**Contingency**: If change requested, PM evaluates effort (should be <4 hours); approve if within 5% contingency budget

**Result**: Risk accepted; no mitigation cost incurred; contingency funds available if needed
