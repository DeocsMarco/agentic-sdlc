## Purpose

This form guides the scoring and prioritization of identified risks using a Probability × Impact matrix. It calculates risk scores (1-9 scale) to prioritize which risks require immediate mitigation planning and which can be monitored. This ensures project resources focus on the highest priority risks.

**Used By**: Project Manager (with input from project team and stakeholders)
**Time Required**: 30-45 minutes (after risks identified in WORK-PM-001)

---

## Methodology Overview

Risk scoring uses a simple multiplication formula: **Risk Score = Probability × Impact**. Each risk is rated on two dimensions (Probability and Impact) using a 3-point scale (Low/Medium/High), resulting in scores from 1 (Low × Low) to 9 (High × High).

**Key Principles**:
1. **Probability**: How likely is this risk to occur? (Low = <30%, Medium = 30-70%, High = >70%)
2. **Impact**: If it occurs, how severe is the consequence? (Low = minor inconvenience, Medium = significant delay/cost, High = project failure)
3. **Risk Score**: Product of Probability and Impact determines priority

**Steps**:
1. Review each identified risk from WORK-PM-001
2. Rate Probability (H/M/L) based on likelihood
3. Rate Impact (H/M/L) based on severity
4. Calculate Risk Score using scoring matrix
5. Sort risks by score to identify top priorities
6. High-priority risks (score 6-9) require mitigation plans

## Reference Tables

### Table 1: Probability Rating Scale

| Rating | Likelihood | Percentage | Criteria |
|--------|------------|------------|----------|
| **L** (Low) | Unlikely | <30% | Rarely happens; requires unusual circumstances |
| **M** (Medium) | Possible | 30-70% | Could reasonably occur; has happened on past projects |
| **H** (High) | Likely | >70% | Probably will occur; strong indicators present |

**Usage**: For each risk, ask "What's the realistic chance this will happen?" Consider historical data, current indicators, and expert judgment.

---

### Table 2: Impact Rating Scale

| Rating | Severity | Impact on Project | Criteria |
|--------|----------|-------------------|----------|
| **L** (Low) | Minor | <1 week delay OR <5% cost increase | Minor inconvenience; workarounds available; minimal client impact |
| **M** (Medium) | Moderate | 1-4 weeks delay OR 5-15% cost increase | Significant disruption; requires replanning; client notices |
| **H** (High) | Severe | >4 weeks delay OR >15% cost increase OR project failure | Critical impact; jeopardizes success; contract breach risk |

**Usage**: For each risk, ask "If this happens, how bad is the damage?" Consider worst-case scenario, not mitigated outcome.

---

### Table 3: Risk Scoring Matrix

|  | **Impact: Low (1)** | **Impact: Medium (2)** | **Impact: High (3)** |
|---|---------------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| **Probability: Low (1)** | **1** - Monitor | **2** - Monitor | **3** - Active |
| **Probability: Medium (2)** | **2** - Monitor | **4** - Active | **6** - Urgent |
| **Probability: High (3)** | **3** - Active | **6** - Urgent | **9** - Critical |

**Priority Thresholds**:
- **Score 1-2**: Monitor only (review monthly; no active mitigation unless risk changes)
- **Score 3-4**: Active monitoring (review bi-weekly; consider low-cost mitigation)
- **Score 6**: Urgent (mitigation plan required; assign owner; track weekly)
- **Score 9**: Critical (immediate mitigation; executive awareness; daily tracking)

**Usage**: Find intersection of Probability and Impact ratings to determine Risk Score.

## Common Scoring Errors & Solutions

| Error | Cause | Solution |
|-------|-------|----------|
| All risks rated "High" probability | Fear-based thinking; lack of data | Use historical data; ask "Has this actually happened before?" |
| Impact underestimated | Optimism bias; not considering cascade effects | Ask "What's the worst case if this happens?" Consider secondary impacts |
| Disagreement on ratings between team members | Different perspectives or information | Discuss rationale openly; use voting or defer to subject matter expert |
| Scores cluster around medium (all 4s) | Avoiding extremes; lack of differentiation | Force rank: "Which is truly higher priority?" Use comparative analysis |
| Probability and impact confused | Mixing likelihood with severity | Separate questions: First "Will it happen?" Then "If so, how bad?" |

## Example Risk Scores

### High-Priority Examples (Mitigation Required)

**Example 1**: "Key developer leaves mid-project"
- Probability: H (3) - Developer has competing offer, historically 40% turnover in this role
- Impact: H (3) - 6-week delay to replace and ramp up; jeopardizes delivery date
- **Score: 9 (Critical)** - Immediate knowledge transfer and succession plan needed

**Example 2**: "Third-party API unreliable during peak load"
- Probability: M (2) - API vendor has 95% uptime SLA; occasional outages reported
- Impact: H (3) - System unusable during outage; contractual SLA breach risk
- **Score: 6 (Urgent)** - Implement caching and fallback mechanism

### Low-Priority Examples (Monitor Only)

**Example 3**: "Minor UI styling inconsistency across browsers"
- Probability: M (2) - Cross-browser issues common but usually minor
- Impact: L (1) - Cosmetic only; does not affect functionality
- **Score: 2 (Monitor)** - Test on major browsers; fix if time permits

**Example 4**: "Client requests minor wording changes to documentation"
- Probability: L (1) - Client typically satisfied with initial drafts
- Impact: L (1) - 1-2 hours to update; no timeline impact
- **Score: 1 (Monitor)** - Track but no proactive action needed
